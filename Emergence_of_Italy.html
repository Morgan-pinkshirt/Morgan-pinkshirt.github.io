Conditions in Italy post-WW1

Political conditions:
The government is seen as incompetent due to its lack of actions to help the Italian population to afford food and to find a job 5.

Economic conditions:
In 1919, there are over 2 million unemployed Italians6. As a result, over 1 million of workers go on several strikes between the early 1919 until the end of 19207.

Social conditions:
In early 1920s., the living standards of most Italians increases8. However, between 1927 and 1931, the wages are cut by 25% to 40% therefore, the number of unemployed workers triples9. Consequently, living standards must decrease.

Mussolini's rise

Mussolini's Fascist Groups
In March 1919, Mussolini forms the Fasci di Combattimento , which translates to 'Fighting' or 'Battle Group', to regroup the different right-wing groups together10. Even though the Fasci di Combattimento are spread across Italy in about 70 towns, no fascist candidates are elected in the November 1919 elections11. Mussolini creates the squadre d'azione, or 'action squads', controlled by local fascist leaders called ras12. He sells these groups to landowners and factory owners to deal with the strikes13. However, the squadre d'azione, composed of demobilized army officers and middle class students, believes in violence rather than in any coherent political ideology, therefore, there is a lack of organization14.

The militia, recognizable by their blackshirt, comes from all over Italy. The purpose of the March is to make it look like a coup d'état15. That way, Mussolini's supporters could demonstrate his power and ability to take over the government.

March to Rome
On October 16, 1922, Mussolini co-ordinates The March to Rome which results in a group of 40 000 strong national militia walking to Rome16. As a consequence, the government arrests the militia and calls for a state of emergency17. However, the king, Victor Emmanuel III does not like that idea due to his doubts on the army's loyalty18. The Prime Minester resignes and Mussolini is appointed to replace him19.

Formation of Fascist movement

In October 1921, the Fasci di Combattimento is renamed to the Partito Nazionale Fascista, which translates to the National Fascist Party (PNF)20. Afterwards, Mussolini changes its most extreme ideas to reduce the anger of the Roman Catholic Church and the worries of the upper and middle classes21. Because his position is still unstable, he cannot afford protests and rebellions from the population.

Leggi Fascistissime
On December 24, 1925, Mussolini creates the Leggi Fascistissime, also known as the fascist law, to gain more power within the government22. With this law, Mussolini requires to be called Il Dulce, or 'The Leader'23. This also grants him more power such as the ability to pass new laws without approval which ultimately suppresses the opposition, and tightens censorship rules24.

Romanità
Mussolini also promotes a movement called the Romanità, or the 'Romanness'25. He aims to broaden the population's appeal to fascism by recreating the Roman Empire and its greatness26.
